Cold Email Templates for PhD, Master's & Research Internships

Sending your first email to a professor can feel intimidating. Many students worry about what to write, how long the email should be, whether they should attach a CV, or whether the professor will even read it. The truth is that professors receive hundreds of emails every semester. A short, well-written and personalized email has a much better chance of receiving a response than a long generic message copied from the internet. This guide explains how to write effective cold emails, shows a real example, and provides templates that you can adapt for your own applications.

Important

Do not copy templates word for word. Use them only as a reference. Every email should be personalized for the professor you are contacting.

Choosing a Good Subject Line

Your subject line is the first thing a professor sees. Avoid vague subjects such as:

  • Need PhD
  • Hello Professor
  • Please Help Me
  • Research Opportunity

Instead, briefly mention your purpose and research area. Examples include:

  • Prospective PhD Student – Stellar Astrophysics
  • Research Internship Inquiry – Computational Astrophysics
  • Seeking Research Opportunities in Machine Learning and Astronomy
  • Prospective Master's Student Interested in Exoplanets

Example Cold Email

The following email is based on a real research inquiry. Personal details have been generalized so you can focus on the structure rather than the individual applicant.

Subject: Seeking Research Opportunities in Stellar Astrophysics and Machine Learning Dear Professor [Name], I hope you are doing well. My name is [Your Name], and I recently completed my Master's degree in Physics at [University]. My primary research interests are computational stellar astrophysics and the application of machine learning to stellar evolution. For my Master's thesis, I worked on accreting white dwarfs using the MESA stellar evolution code. My research focused on how time-dependent accretion histories influence thermonuclear burning and recurrent nova evolution. In addition to my thesis, I completed a research internship involving observational time-series analysis of recurrent novae. This project introduced me to machine learning and motivated me to explore how modern AI techniques can be applied to astrophysical problems. While learning more about this area, I read your recent publications on [Research Topic]. I found your work particularly interesting because it combines stellar astrophysics with modern machine-learning methods. Your research closely matches the direction I would like to pursue during my PhD. I am planning to apply for PhD positions next year and would greatly appreciate any advice regarding potential opportunities in your group. If there is a possibility to contribute to one of your projects, I would be grateful for the opportunity to learn and contribute. I have attached my CV for your review. Thank you for your time and consideration. Kind regards, [Your Name]

Why This Email Works

A good cold email is not successful because it is long. It is successful because every paragraph has a clear purpose. Let's examine each section.

1. Short Introduction

The professor immediately learns who you are, where you studied and what your research interests are. There is no unnecessary personal story or unrelated information.

2. Research Experience

Instead of simply saying "I worked on astrophysics," the email briefly explains the research problem, methods and scientific context. This demonstrates genuine research experience rather than simply listing projects.

3. Why This Professor?

One of the strongest parts of the example email is that it clearly explains why the professor was contacted. Instead of writing generic statements such as "I am interested in your excellent research," the email refers to the professor's recent publications and explains why those papers are relevant to the student's own interests. This demonstrates that the email was written specifically for that professor and was not sent to hundreds of researchers.

Pro Tip

Before emailing a professor, spend at least 30 minutes reading their university profile and two or three of their recent papers. Mention one idea, result or project that genuinely interested you.

4. The Closing Paragraph

Notice that the email does not directly ask:

  • "Can you accept me?"
  • "Can you fund me?"
  • "Please give me a PhD."

Instead, it politely expresses interest in future opportunities and asks whether there may be projects available. This approach is much more professional and respectful of the professor's time.

Attach Your CV

Always attach a well-formatted academic CV unless the professor specifically requests otherwise. Your CV allows the professor to quickly review your academic background without asking for additional information.

Reusable Cold Email Template

Subject: Prospective PhD Student Interested in [Research Area] Dear Professor [Last Name], I hope you are doing well. My name is [Your Name], and I am currently completing my [Bachelor's/Master's Degree] in [Subject] at [University]. My current research focuses on [Research Topic]. During my recent project, I worked on [Brief Description] and gained experience with [Important Skills]. While exploring research groups working in this area, I read your recent publication on [Paper Topic]. I found your work particularly interesting because [Specific Reason]. I am planning to apply for [PhD/Master's/Research Internship] opportunities and was wondering whether you expect to recruit students in your research group. I have attached my CV for your reference. I would greatly appreciate any advice or information regarding future opportunities. Thank you very much for your time and consideration. Kind regards, [Your Name]

Common Cold Email Mistakes

  • Sending exactly the same email to every professor.
  • Writing extremely long emails.
  • Using AI-generated text without personalizing it.
  • Not reading the professor's research before contacting them.
  • Writing vague subject lines.
  • Attaching unnecessary files.
  • Asking questions already answered on the laboratory website.
  • Using informal language.
  • Expecting an immediate reply.
Remember

Professors are usually very busy. A polite, concise and personalized email is much more likely to receive a response than a long message describing every detail of your academic history.

Should You Follow Up?

Yes—but only if you do it professionally. If you do not receive a reply after about one week, it is perfectly acceptable to send a short follow-up email. Keep it brief and polite. Sometimes emails are missed, buried under many other messages, or forgotten during busy periods. A follow-up serves as a gentle reminder rather than a second request.

Subject: Follow-up: Prospective PhD Student Interested in Stellar Astrophysics Dear Professor [Last Name], I hope you are doing well. I wanted to briefly follow up regarding my previous email about potential research opportunities in your group. I understand that you are very busy, but I wanted to check whether you had the opportunity to review my message. Thank you once again for your time and consideration. Kind regards, [Your Name]
Do Not Spam

One follow-up after approximately one week is usually enough. If you still do not receive a response, it is best to move on and contact other professors rather than sending repeated emails.

Final Advice

A successful cold email is not about using impressive vocabulary or writing a very long message. Instead, it should clearly answer four questions:

  • Who are you?
  • What research experience do you have?
  • Why are you contacting this specific professor?
  • What are you hoping to do next?

If you can answer these questions honestly and professionally, you will already have a stronger email than many applicants.
